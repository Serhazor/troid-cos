Troid Cos

The Ancient Irish Art of Seated Leg Wrestling

“May your grip be strong, and your shin unbroken.” – Saint Mac Dáire of the Rocks

Historical Origins

Troid Cos (“leg fight”) is an ancient Irish martial art, dating back to circa 500 AD. Practiced by Celtic monks and warrior-poets, it originated in stone huts along the western coast. Warriors recovering from battle developed it as a way to spar without standing — or damaging the thatch roof.

The Scrolls of Gleann na gCromá describe “the seated duel of limbs, where honour is taken from the shin and returned by tap.”

Traditional Setup

  • Participants begin seated, knees bent, heels planted.
  • Arms and legs may be used for gripping, framing, defending, and finishing.
  • Standing up is grounds for disqualification — unless celebrating a win.

Historically fought on woven rush mats or, in modern times, tatami.

Legal Techniques

Only lower-body submissions are considered pure. Arms may assist, but upper-body finishes are frowned upon unless part of an “Offering the Branch.”

  • Celtic Corkscrew – heel hook
  • Druid’s Knot – straight ankle lock
  • Fionnán’s Fork – knee bar
  • Tuatha Trap – sankaku knee crush
  • Lough Sweep – inside leg reap and shin lever
  • Bog Clamp – double outside ashi, rare and respected

Scoring (if no submission)

  • Clean sweep: 2 pts
  • Dominant entanglement: 2 pts
  • Visible distress from submission: 1 pt
  • Escape and re-control: 1 pt
  • Standing or disengaging: Disqualified

Match Format

  • One round, 4 minutes
  • Victory by tap, verbal (“Mo chosa!”), or points
  • Draw: Sudden Death Stone Flip (winner picks top or bottom)

Champion’s Honour

  • A Carved Shinstone (symbol of mastery)
  • Woolen wraps dyed with bog myrtle
  • Entry into the Order of the Spiral Toes

Every 7 years, the Grand Clash of Seated Kin crowns the High Shin atop Slieve na gCos — whose leg must never wear a boot again.

Modern Revival

Banned by Normans in 1179 for “excessive leg independence,” Troid Cos is making a return. Grapplers, folklorists, and rural gyms alike are reviving it via YouTube footage, ogham stones, and intuition.
